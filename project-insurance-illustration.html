Modernizing Insurance Quoting for 2,500 Advisors: Build vs. Partner Decision

Led the modernization of RBC's legacy insurance illustration software serving 2,500 advisors. Developed a decision framework to evaluate vendor partnerships, balancing security, compliance, and intellectual property protection while delivering a cloud platform that eliminated offline constraints and brought RBC up to speed with competitors like Manulife and IA Financial.

Client

RBC Insurance

Date completed

December 2024

Role

Product Manager, RBC

The Challenge Strategic Decision Framework Requirements & Architecture The Solution Impact & Learnings

The Challenge

RBC's insurance illustration software served 2,500 advisors but ran entirely offline on individual desktops. Advisors couldn't collaborate on complex quotes—they had to email raw files back and forth. Comparing multiple scenarios side-by-side was clunky. Work was trapped on single devices, so if a laptop died, quotes were lost. Meanwhile, competitors like Manulife and IA Financial had modern cloud platforms with real-time collaboration and multi-device access.

Why This Became a Priority: Advisor feedback surveys showed growing frustration with the outdated interface and offline limitations. 40% of advisors cited "inability to work remotely" as a top pain point, and 35% reported losing work due to device failures. More critically, we were hearing from the field that advisors were choosing to sell competitors' products because their quoting tools were easier to use. This was a direct threat to product sales.

Through advisor interviews and usability testing, I identified the core problems: inability to collaborate on complex cases (advisors emailing files back and forth), difficult scenario comparison, work trapped on individual devices, and perception that the outdated UI signaled less reliable tools. The offline architecture was the root cause of most issues.

Decision Framework: Build vs. Partner

Build vs. Partner Decision: We had two options: build the platform in-house or partner with a vendor. Building in-house would give us full control but would take 18+ months with our engineering capacity. Partnering with an external vendor specializing in insurance illustration software would get us to market in 4 months using their pre-built components.

I recommended the vendor partnership. The trade-off was clear: we'd sacrifice some design control and pay licensing fees, but we'd get to market 14 months faster and stop losing advisors to competitors. Speed was more valuable than perfection.

However, the vendor's pre-built components didn't match RBC's design system. Using them as-is would create an inconsistent experience. This introduced a second decision: leverage their components for speed, or customize them to maintain our design standards?

I developed a framework evaluating three approaches for sharing RBC's Interface Guidelines (RIG) with the vendor. The core question: should we give them our actual React components, or just the styling specifications?

Option 1: Manually download RIG component code and hand it to the vendor
Option 2: Create a mirrored repository that auto-updates when RIG updates
Option 3: Provide only styling files and let the vendor restyle their existing components

The analysis examined trade-offs across security risk, compliance requirements, ongoing maintenance, design consistency, future transferability, intellectual property protection, sustainability, implementation speed, and scalability.

Success Criteria (Defined Upfront):

  • • Achieve 80%+ advisor adoption within 6 months of launch
  • • Reach 4.0+ satisfaction score (out of 5) on usability surveys
  • • Enable multi-device access for 100% of advisors
  • • Reduce quote generation time by 30%
  • • Zero data loss incidents from device failures

After analyzing the options, I recommended "Vendor Components with RIG Styling" to business leadership. This approach balanced development speed with security by sharing only visual design specifications rather than proprietary code, while maintaining good design consistency and low compliance overhead. The recommendation prioritized fast implementation and intellectual property protection over perfect design consistency, accepting that minor component behavior differences were an acceptable trade-off for reduced security risk and faster time-to-market.

Stakeholder Buy-in: Getting alignment required navigating competing priorities. Security wanted zero code sharing. Design wanted pixel-perfect consistency. Engineering wanted minimal maintenance overhead. Legal wanted airtight IP protection. I presented the decision framework to all stakeholders, showing how Option 3 balanced everyone's top concerns without satisfying anyone completely—which is usually the sign of a good compromise. The VP of Distribution championed the project after I showed how advisor churn risk justified the investment.

The Solution

The modernized platform delivered a contemporary interface with cloud-based functionality, bringing RBC to competitive parity while enabling workflows previously impossible with offline architecture.

Key Platform Improvements:

  • Modern Design System: Clean interface aligned with RBC's design guidelines, eliminating the perception of outdated tools
  • Cloud Architecture: Multi-device access enabling advisors to start quotes in office and complete at client sites without interruption
  • Collaborative Workflows: Multiple advisors can work together on complex illustrations, enabling senior-junior mentorship
  • Scenario Comparison: Side-by-side quote comparison tools for presenting options to clients during consultations
  • Integrated Application Flow: Seamless quote-to-application handoff with auto-populated client data

Implementation Approach: Over 4 months (August-December 2024), I managed delivery across multiple teams—internal QA, internal design, vendor development team, and vendor technical experts. I ran weekly syncs to keep everyone aligned, coordinated requirements delivery, managed design asset handoff through Storybook and Artifactory, and handled integration between the new cloud platform and existing RBC systems. By choosing Option 3 (styling-only approach), we avoided lengthy legal approvals and security reviews, enabling faster deployment while maintaining acceptable design consistency.

What I'd Do Differently

Post-launch research revealed gaps I should have caught earlier. Third-party advisors struggled to find software upgrade links—we designed for internal advisors and forgot about the broker channel. Bugs in updates occasionally broke quote generation, which taught me to push harder for staging environments and better QA processes with vendors. DI and Universal Life products remained more complex than Life insurance, contributing to lower satisfaction scores. I should have run more usability tests on those specific products before launch.

The biggest surprise: Advisors showed split preferences between web-based and desktop versions. Web users loved always-updated rates, but desktop users wanted offline capability for client meetings in areas with poor internet. We assumed everyone would prefer cloud, but that wasn't true. If I could redo this, I'd have validated that assumption earlier and potentially built a hybrid solution.

Feature requests captured included direct links from quoting tool to software updates, tooltips explaining product differences, enhanced occupation lookup, improved DI discount information, save and resume functionality, and better reporting options. These findings informed the V2 roadmap, prioritizing highest-impact improvements based on request frequency and business value.

Reducing Insurance Application Time by 50% Through Platform Consolidation

Led product strategy and cross-functional development of a unified Salesforce platform consolidating Critical Illness, Life, and Disability insurance products. The Challenge

Insurance Advisors selling RBC Insurance spent 2 hours per application manually re-entering the same client data across four disconnected platforms. This caused 20% error rates, frustrated advisors, and lost deals to competitors with streamlined processes. No data carried over between systems.

Why This Became a Priority: Brokerage advisors—who represent 40% of our distribution channel—were actively choosing competitors with simpler application processes. We were losing market share in a critical segment, and advisor satisfaction scores had dropped to their lowest point in 3 years.

Product Strategy & Solution

Vision: Create a universal e-application platform where advisors can process all three insurance products through a single system that adapts to client information and product selections.

My Approach:
Rather than building three separate solutions or creating a rigid, one-size-fits-all system, I defined requirements for a modular architecture powered by a rules engine. This would allow:

  • • Each product to maintain its unique eligibility criteria and workflows
  • • The platform to intelligently combine products based on client selections
  • • Business teams to update rules without engineering intervention
  • • The system to scale to future products without rebuilds

Core Product Requirements:
I mapped requirements across six problem areas identified in research:

  • Unified workflow - Single platform eliminating the 4-system shuffle
  • Intelligent data handling - Auto-population from Salesforce, zero duplicate entry
  • Real-time validation - Clear error feedback preventing downstream rework
  • Progress visibility - Status tracking reducing client anxiety
  • Privacy-first design - Phased information collection with transparent explanations
  • Consistent experience - Unified UI/UX regardless of product combination

Success Criteria (Defined Upfront):

  • • Reduce application processing time from 2 hours to under 1 hour
  • • Achieve 90%+ advisor adoption within 6 months of launch
  • • Decrease data entry errors by at least 50%
  • • Increase multi-product application submissions by 15%
  • • Improve advisor satisfaction scores from 6.2 to 8.0+ (out of 10)

Key Trade-offs & Decisions:

  • Salesforce vs. Custom Build: Chose Salesforce despite higher licensing costs because it gave us faster time-to-market (9 months vs. 18+ months) and leveraged existing CRM data. The trade-off was less UI flexibility, but speed to market was more critical.
  • What We Didn't Build: Deprioritized mobile app and offline mode in V1 to focus on the core web experience. Research showed 95% of advisors worked from laptops, so mobile could wait for V2.
  • Phased Rollout: Launched with Life and Critical Illness first, added Disability 3 months later. This let us validate the architecture with lower-complexity products before tackling DI's intricate occupation-based pricing.

Product Architecture & Technical Approach

The solution required deep business analysis before any technical design. I created a comprehensive matrix documenting eligibility criteria across all three products—age limits, citizenship requirements, health conditions, occupation restrictions, and product dependencies. This analysis revealed which elements could be shared and which required product-specific handling.

Unified Workflow with Modular Product Configurations

Technical Requirements Defined:
I partnered with engineering to define requirements for:

  • Rules engine - Real-time eligibility checking eliminating invalid product combinations upfront
  • Modular product architecture - Each insurance product as an independent, configurable component
  • Conditional logic system - Dynamic field visibility based on product selections and client data
  • API integrations - Two-way data sync with Salesforce and illustration software
  • Automated compliance checks - Built-in regulatory validation across all workflows
  • Scalable configuration - Flexible foundation accommodating future products without system overhauls

Key Decision: Building a rules engine that business teams could maintain was critical. This wasn't just about solving today's problem—it was about creating a platform that could evolve with changing regulations and business needs without requiring engineering resources for every update.

Stakeholder Management & Getting Buy-in

This project required alignment across 5 business units with competing priorities. Here's how I navigated it:

Executive Sponsorship

Presented business case to VP of Distribution showing projected multi-million dollar annual savings from reduced processing time and significant revenue upside from improved advisor retention. Secured executive sponsor who championed the project in leadership meetings.

Product Owners

Each insurance line (Life, CI, DI) had separate product owners who initially wanted their own custom solutions. I ran workshops mapping their unique requirements and showed how a shared platform could serve all three without compromising product-specific needs. The rules engine was key to getting their buy-in.

Compliance & Legal

Brought them in early to review architecture. Their biggest concern was ensuring regulatory requirements were baked into the system, not bolted on later. Made compliance validation a core architectural requirement, which earned their support.

Engineering

Faced pushback on timeline—they wanted 18 months, I had 9. Negotiated by agreeing to phased rollout and cutting scope (no mobile V1). Weekly sync meetings kept us aligned on technical feasibility vs. business needs.

Learnings & Reflections

1. Understanding the business deeply saves time later
I spent weeks mapping out eligibility rules across all three insurance products. This revealed consolidation opportunities that wouldn't have been obvious if I'd jumped straight to wireframes. That upfront work saved us months of rework when we realized the products shared more logic than anyone thought.

2. Letting business teams control their own rules was a game-changer
The best decision we made was building a system where business teams could update product rules without waiting for engineering. When regulations changed or new products launched, they could make updates themselves in hours instead of waiting weeks for a developer.

3. Track everything from day one
We built analytics into the platform from launch, not as an afterthought. This let us see exactly where advisors got stuck, which fields caused the most errors, and which features went unused. We didn't just ship and move on—we kept improving based on real usage data.

4. The best ideas come from listening to everyone
Working closely with engineers, designers, compliance officers, and advisors taught me that good solutions come from understanding everyone's constraints. Engineers knew what was technically feasible, compliance knew what was legally required, and advisors knew what would actually work in the field. My job was to synthesize all of that, not dictate requirements from a vacuum.

Context

Creditor insurance is a financial protection product offered by RBC Insurance to help bank clients safeguard their major purchases and credit commitments. When customers apply for mortgages, loans, or lines of credit, they have the option to purchase insurance that continues making payments on their behalf in case of disability, death, critical illness, or job loss. This protection ensures that clients won't lose their homes or damage their credit during life's unexpected events, while also reducing risk for the bank.

The Challenge

The creditor insurance integration faced a fundamental organizational challenge: two departments with competing priorities and conflicting ownership models. In the current experience, the Banking department owned the entire customer flow for line of credit and mortgage applications—from start to finish. This meant that any time the Insurance department needed to make changes to insurance-related steps within that flow, they had to wait for Banking team prioritization—creating bottlenecks and delays.

Why This Became a Priority: We were releasing updates to the creditor insurance product—new coverage options, pricing changes, and improved disclosures. Every update required going through the Banking team's development queue, which meant weeks of delays. This was costing us revenue—we couldn't respond quickly to market changes or regulatory requirements. The Insurance team needed autonomy to iterate independently.

The Solution: Widget Architecture

Decision: Inflow Widget Approach

I recommended the inflow widget approach despite pushback from UX, Architecture, and Development teams who argued it would create more complexity due to shared screen requirements. I prioritized conversion rates and user experience over technical simplicity.

Key Rationale:

  • Historical Data: Previous projects showed in-flow insurance placement converts 20-25% better than post-flow. Users are engaged and in "financial decision mode" during banking tasks.
  • Better User Experience: Insurance feels like a natural part of the banking process rather than an afterthought, increasing perceived value and relevance.
  • Timing Matters: Users are most receptive to additional financial products during their primary banking task, not after they've mentally completed it.
  • Manageable Trade-offs: Yes, shared screens add complexity, but the conversion uplift justifies it. We could address the technical challenges through careful architecture.

Stakeholder Buy-in: UX, Architecture, and Dev teams initially preferred the simpler post-activation approach. I presented historical conversion data from similar projects and ran the business case: even with higher development costs, the 20%+ conversion uplift would pay back the investment in 3 months. The Insurance VP championed the approach after seeing the revenue projections.

Inflow Integration Architecture

Implementation Strategy

To address the technical challenges while maintaining departmental autonomy, I designed:

Shared Screen Components

  • Created shared screens for document review (Step 5) and final completion (Step 6)
  • Allowed both departments to maintain their experiences within a unified flow
  • Established clear ownership boundaries

Independent Insurance Screens

  • Steps 4a-4b fully owned by Insurance team
  • Enabled independent iteration without Banking coordination
  • Maintained widget encapsulation

Minimal Banking Development

  • Focused on simple integration points rather than custom implementations
  • Reduced Banking team burden while enabling Insurance autonomy

Impact & Results

Insurance Team Autonomy

Reduced time-to-ship for insurance updates from 6+ weeks to under 2 weeks. Insurance team can now iterate independently without waiting for Banking prioritization, enabling faster response to market changes and regulatory requirements.

Improved Conversion Rates

In-flow placement achieved 22% higher insurance conversion rate compared to post-activation alternatives, consistent with historical data from similar projects. Users engaged during active banking tasks are more likely to add insurance.

Better User Experience

Improved price transparency, simplified buying experience, and created product-agnostic architecture that works across all creditor insurance types (life, disability, critical illness, job loss).

Technical Architecture

Designed shared screen architecture for document review and completion phases, maintaining clear ownership boundaries between Banking and Insurance systems while enabling integrated user experience.

What I'd Do Differently

I should have involved Dev and Architecture earlier in the decision process. They raised valid concerns about shared screen complexity that I initially dismissed. While the inflow approach was the right call, earlier collaboration would have surfaced technical constraints sooner and built more buy-in. I also underestimated the ongoing maintenance burden of shared screens—something to consider for future cross-department integrations.

Background

Background

I earned my Bachelor of Pharmacy with distinction from Obafemi Awolowo University before transitioning into tech through self-teaching. As a pharmacist, I counseled patients on complex medication regimens, learning to explain medical concepts in ways anyone could understand. This skill now shapes how I communicate product requirements to stakeholders and design user experiences.

My Journey

My Journey

My journey from healthcare to technology has been driven by solving problems that affect real people. Working with chronic disease patients, I learned to ask why they weren't taking their medications—was it cost, side effects, or just forgetting? This taught me to dig into the real problem, not just the symptom. I later joined a healthcare design agency where I led the design of a gamified medication adherence app, achieving an 18% increase in compliance for TB and HIV patients in Nigeria.

I moved to Canada and worked as a Product Designer for an employee recognition platform, where I learned to build products at scale. But I wanted more strategic impact—to not just design solutions, but define what problems we should solve and why. That led me to join Royal Bank of Canada as a Product Manager.

At RBC, I led multiple product initiatives—consolidating insurance platforms that reduced application processing time by 50%, and modernizing quoting software for 2,500 advisors. The experience reinforced that great products aren't just about features—they're about understanding how people work and designing solutions that make their jobs easier.

At USC Marshall, I'm building the business strategy and technical depth to lead products at scale. I'm focused on go-to-market strategy, pricing, and understanding how to make the business case for product decisions—skills I didn't get as a designer or early PM.

My PM Philosophy

My PM Philosophy

I build products by understanding the problem first, not jumping to solutions. The best features are the ones users actually adopt—which means deeply understanding their workflows, measuring what matters, and iterating based on real feedback.

I prioritize impact over output. That means saying no to features that don't move the needle, getting stakeholder buy-in through data, and enabling teams to ship quickly. I've learned that the best decisions come from understanding the constraints—technical, business, and user—and finding the path that balances all three.
